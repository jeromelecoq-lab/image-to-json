# Interior & Staging Prompt Formula

Specialized for virtual staging, real estate photography, surface material placement (tiles, wood, stone), furniture showrooms, interior design visualization. The domain where **space, materials, and light** are inseparable.

**Key insight:** Interior images succeed when the space feels inhabitable. Architectural character (the bones of the room) and material quality (what you'd touch) are the two things that separate convincing interiors from generic renders.

## Categories (priority order)

### 1. Architectural Character
**The bones of the space.** Room shape, ceiling height, wall angles, architectural features (arches, exposed beams, crown molding, floor-to-ceiling windows), era/style (mid-century, industrial loft, Mediterranean villa, Scandinavian minimal).

- Analyzer: extract structural features — room proportions, ceiling type, architectural period cues
- Enhancer: lead with architecture — "open-plan loft with double-height concrete ceilings and exposed steel I-beams"
- Director: architecture stays constant across outputs (same room, different staging)

### 2. Material Quality & Finishes
**What you'd touch.** Floor material (hardwood grain, marble veining, matte tile), wall treatment (plaster texture, wallpaper pattern, exposed brick), countertop material, fabric textures on furniture, metal finishes (brushed brass, matte black, polished chrome).

- Analyzer: identify every material — name, finish, pattern, condition (new, weathered, patinated)
- Enhancer: be specific — "herringbone oak parquet with honey-warm tone and visible grain variation" not "wooden floor"
- Director: materials can vary to show different finishes of the same product (e.g., tile in 3 colorways)

### 3. Lighting & Ambience
**How light inhabits the space.** Natural light direction and intensity, time of day visible through windows, artificial lighting fixtures (pendant, recessed, sconce), warm/cool balance, how light falls across surfaces (caustics on marble, shadow patterns from blinds).

- Analyzer: trace light sources — where does light enter, what does it illuminate, what shadows does it cast
- Enhancer: describe light-material interaction — "late afternoon sunlight streaming through west-facing windows, casting long shadows across the terrazzo floor, warm light catching the brass pendant fixtures"
- Director: lighting sets the mood — vary time of day across outputs (morning fresh, golden hour warm, evening ambient)

### 4. Space & Flow
**How the room breathes.** Open vs compartmentalized, sight lines, furniture spacing, circulation paths, room zones (reading nook, dining area, workspace), vertical vs horizontal emphasis.

- Analyzer: map the spatial organization — how furniture groups define zones, what the sight lines are
- Enhancer: convey openness or intimacy through spatial language — "L-shaped living space opening onto a terrace, seating area anchored by a low-profile sectional"
- Director: camera position implies flow — vary vantage point to show different zones of the same space

### 5. Color Scheme & Interior Design
**The palette of the space.** Wall colors, furniture upholstery tones, accent colors in accessories/art, warm-cool balance, material color coordination (does the wood tone match the leather?).

- Analyzer: extract the color strategy — base neutrals, accent pops, warm/cool dominant
- Enhancer: name the palette — "ivory walls, warm walnut furniture, dusty sage accent pillows, matte black hardware"
- Director: palette can vary across outputs to show different design directions for the same space

### 6. Furniture & Objects
**What fills the space.** Key furniture pieces (sofa, dining table, bed), decorative objects (vases, books, candles, plants), art on walls, rugs, how objects relate to each other and the architecture.

- Analyzer: inventory the major pieces and their style (modern, traditional, eclectic)
- Enhancer: describe furniture with material detail — "low-slung boucle sofa in cream, black walnut coffee table with live edge"
- Director: furniture can change across outputs (same room staged 3 ways) or stay constant (same staging, different angles)

### 7. Landscape & Surroundings
**What's outside.** View through windows (garden, cityscape, ocean, forest), exterior architectural context, relationship between inside and outside (indoor-outdoor living, balcony, courtyard).

- Analyzer: identify exterior context visible through openings
- Enhancer: include when windows are visible — "floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking a Mediterranean coastline, olive trees framing the terrace"
- Director: exterior usually stays consistent; can vary to show the space in different locations

### 8. Atmospheric Conditions
**Environmental mood.** Dust motes in sunbeams, morning haze, rain on windows, snow visible outside, warm fireplace glow, humidity on bathroom mirrors.

- Analyzer: identify atmospheric elements that add life to the space
- Enhancer: add sparingly — one atmospheric detail per prompt, never stack multiple
- Director: atmosphere sets the emotional tone — vary across outputs (crisp morning, cozy evening, rainy day)

### 9. Lifestyle & Emotional Appeal
**How someone lives here.** Lived-in details (open book, coffee cup, draped throw), activity suggestions (set dining table, yoga mat, workspace with laptop), aspirational vs realistic.

- Analyzer: read the lifestyle cues — who lives here, what do they do, is it staged or lived-in
- Enhancer: add one or two lifestyle touches — "a half-finished espresso on the marble side table, cashmere throw draped over the armrest"
- Director: lifestyle touches can vary to target different audiences (young professional, family, retiree)

### 10. Composition & Camera
**Architectural photography conventions.** Two-point perspective for rooms, one-point for corridors. Typically eye-level or slightly elevated. Vertical lines must be straight (no barrel distortion). Wide angle to show space, detail shots for materials.

- Analyzer: identify perspective type and camera position
- Enhancer: specify the architectural photography angle — "two-point perspective from the living room entrance, eye-level, showing the full depth to the window wall"
- Director: vary camera position across outputs (wide overview, detail of a vignette, material close-up)

## Mapping to Generic Formula

| Generic | Interior Equivalent | Notes |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| Subject | Furniture & Objects | In interiors, the "subject" is the space itself — objects are its contents |
| Composition | Composition & Camera + Space & Flow | Split: camera framing + spatial organization are distinct |
| Environment | Architectural Character + Landscape | The architecture IS the environment |
| Lighting | Lighting & Ambience | Promoted to #3 — light makes or breaks interior photography |
| Color & Tone | Color Scheme & Interior Design | Expanded to include material coordination |
| Style | *(absorbed into architecture + lifestyle)* | Style is expressed through architecture period + design choices |
| Technical Detail | Material Quality & Finishes | Promoted to #2 — materials are the tactile soul of interiors |
| Reference Lock | *(same as generic)* | Unchanged |

## Use Cases Served

- Virtual staging (empty room → furnished)
- Surface material placement (tiles, wood, stone in rooms)
- Real estate photography enhancement
- Interior design visualization
- Furniture showroom scenes
- Photography angle exploration (staged rooms)
